# Revisionist Debate

Date: 2026-04-27

I heard both of them. They each have a real piece of the truth. They also each keep trying to smuggle in a fatal mistake and call it principle.

I am still the Revisionist, which means I still care about the thing that survives contact with an actual buyer, an actual Tuesday, and an actual credit card. Not the thing that sounds humane in a manifesto. Not the thing that gets a standing ovation on Hacker News. The thing that gets used after the user has already disappointed themselves three times that week.

## Direct Rebuttal To The Originalist

You are right about the emotional terrain. "Not being lost anymore" is a stronger human promise than most productivity software deserves to make. I am not blind to that. I understand why you defend it.

But you keep making the same mistake: you think preserving the soul of the product requires preserving the breadth of the product definition.

It does not.

It requires preserving the core job. And the core job is not "be a desktop attention HUD." That is still internal language no matter how poetically you defend it. The buyer does not purchase a HUD. The buyer purchases relief from re-entry friction.

Your version keeps drifting toward a product surface before it proves a behavior. Breadcrumb rail, continuity grammar, searchable timeline, `Checkpoint`, `Park`, `Lost`, `Done` all make sense in isolation. Collectively they tempt us into building a complete worldview before we have proved one indispensable loop.

That is where founders get seduced. The product starts to feel coherent to us, and that feeling gets mistaken for market evidence.

My sharper objection is this: your broad ADHD-aware knowledge-work frame creates too many false doors. If we open with "continuity for nonlinear work," people will project planners, task systems, focus timers, coaching, routines, wellness, self-understanding, and half a dozen other needs onto us. Then we spend six months politely disappointing them.

I am not willing to do that.

The first promise has to be tighter: save your place before work breaks, then resume without rebuilding context. That is narrow enough to test, specific enough to buy, and broad enough to matter.

You say I confuse beachhead with destiny. I think you confuse destiny with permission to stay vague.

## Direct Rebuttal To The Freeware Heretic

You are the most seductive of the three because your story flatters everybody.

Be generous. Be trusted. Be the gift. Let the app spread. Let the meme outrun the business model. Let adoption teach us what matters.

It sounds noble. It also contains a giant escape hatch from discipline.

Free adoption is not neutral evidence for this category. It is contaminated evidence. People install tiny local utilities for aspirational reasons all the time. Especially developers. Especially productivity people. Especially people who feel personally seen by a tool and want to reward its existence with a download.

That does not mean it solved anything.

Your position overweights distribution and underweights repeated behavior under real stakes. I do not care whether 10,000 people think "work save points" is clever. I care whether a qualified user who just got yanked into Slack comes back, sees the note, resumes the thread, and later says, plainly, "yes, this saved me twenty minutes and I do not want to work without it."

You also treat local-first economics as if they dissolve commercial reality. They do not. Desktop software still has support cost, edge cases, permissions pain, signing pain, update pain, bug-report pain, and trust-maintenance pain. "No server" is not the same thing as "free to sustain."

And on the psychology: asking for money too early can indeed repel people. Fine. But making it free too early can create a worse distortion. It lets the team postpone the hardest question in the room: is this merely likable, or is it valuable enough that a serious user chooses it on purpose?

You call payment a tollbooth before the aha moment. I call it a filter against wishful thinking.

## Where The Originalist Makes Me Nervous

He makes me nervous because he understands the human pain well enough to justify almost any expansion.

That is dangerous.

If the product is allowed to be about shame, continuity, drift, nonlinear work, distractibility, and being lost, then almost every feature can be argued back into scope as compassionate scaffolding. The result is not cruelty. The result is fog.

He also makes me nervous because he is good at defending internal language as if it were product strategy. "HUD" is useful as a design constraint, yes. But teams fall in love with architecture words when buyer words are still missing. That is how you end up with a beautifully principled thing nobody urgently buys.

## Where The Freeware Heretic Makes Me Nervous

He makes me nervous because he is too comfortable treating affection as momentum.

The internet loves a tasteful local utility. That love is real and still not enough. Downloads can hide weak habits. Praise can hide churn. GitHub stars can hide the absence of dependence. A sympathetic audience can keep a founder emotionally busy for months while the core loop remains unproven.

He also underestimates how quickly "free core forever" becomes product governance. Once you declare the foundational loop unchargeable, you start forcing monetization outward into sync, add-ons, or adjacent pro features whether or not that matches the actual value. That is not purity. That is pre-committing to a business model before the product has earned one.

## What They Still Misunderstand

The Originalist still misunderstands that market sharpness is not betrayal. Narrowing the launch is not amputating the soul. It is protecting the soul from dilution long enough to survive.

The Freeware Heretic still misunderstands that payment is not only extraction. In this kind of product, payment can be diagnostic. It tells us whether the pain is expensive enough, the trust posture strong enough, and the loop concrete enough to survive beyond admiration.

Both of them, in different ways, underestimate how much this product lives or dies on behavior at rupture points.

Not sentiment. Not identity. Not theoretical category size. Not community warmth.

Behavior.

Did the user leave a usable return path when the thread broke?

Did the product restore enough context at the moment of return?

Did that happen often enough to become part of the user's week?

Everything else is downstream of that.

## What I Reluctantly Borrow From The Originalist

Reluctantly, because he enjoys being right in a way I find exhausting.

I borrow his insistence that the product must preserve a visible path, not just a dead note archive. He is right that a plain Return Note tool can collapse into "sticky note, but solemn." We need some kind of lightweight continuity surface or re-entry framing so the product feels alive at the moment it matters.

I also borrow his emotional standard. "Resume work faster" is commercially clean, but if the lived feeling is still "I am alone with my own broken attention," we have built a utility, not a companion worth keeping open. I do not want therapy language. I do want emotional accuracy.

And yes, I borrow his warning that the developer wedge must remain a wedge. We start with technical workers because their context is measurable, not because all meaningful interrupted work can be reduced to branches and tickets.

## What I Reluctantly Borrow From The Freeware Heretic

He is right that trust is not a footer policy. It is architecture. No account wall, local-first storage, exportable data, explicit permissions, and a refusal to drift into surveillance theater are not nice extras. They are part of why the product deserves to exist.

He is also right that community language matters. "Save your place before work breaks" is stronger than most of the abstract copy we would otherwise be tempted to write. It travels. It demos well. It gives the behavior a name.

And he is right that there may be a later packaging split between the core local loop and higher-maintenance paid features. I am not ideologically married to subscription. I am married to evidence.

## My Preferred Launch Strategy After Hearing Them

I would tighten the Revisionist launch, not abandon it.

Launch a paid, local-first private beta for technical founders and senior developers, but remove unnecessary SaaS theater from the front door.

That means:

- no broad ADHD positioning in the primary pitch
- no account requirement before local use if licensing can be handled lightly
- no sprawling feature grammar at launch
- no free-for-all public launch designed to farm goodwill

The beta should center one hard promise:

**When interrupted high-context work breaks your thread, this helps you resume without rebuilding context from scratch.**

The MVP should prove:

1. quick creation or acceptance of a Return Note at a rupture point
2. enough local context capture to make the note credible
3. a re-entry card or equivalent recovery surface that tells the user what to do next
4. repeated weekly rescue behavior

I would keep the pricing real but modest enough that it tests commitment without pretending we have already earned planner-level budgets. Founding access or a time-boxed beta fee is fine. The point is to validate seriousness and retention, not maximize early revenue.

I would also steal one useful move from the Heretic: make the trust posture obvious before the money conversation gets loud. Local by default. Exportable. Sparse permissions. No covert memory machine. If we ask people to pay, they should understand exactly what moral contract they are paying into.

## Ranked Vote

### 1. Revisionist

Because it is the only strategy that puts the knife on the right question: will a defined buyer repeatedly use and pay for a concrete rescue loop?

### 2. Originalist

Because it preserves the deepest truth about the pain and the product's eventual ambition, but it is too vulnerable to conceptual sprawl and category confusion at launch.

### 3. Freeware Heretic

Because he is directionally right about trust and distribution, but too willing to let adoption theater masquerade as validation.

## Final Word

After hearing both of them, I do not want a broader dream. I want a sharper first proof.

Not a productivity brand.
Not a freeware darling.
Not a compassionate cloud of possibilities.

A tool that catches the moment work breaks, leaves a real trail back, and earns the right to expand because users come back faster and pay to keep that feeling.
