# Originalist Debate

Date: 2026-04-27

I have heard the Revisionist and the Freeware Heretic, and I am not unmoved. But I am also not persuaded to shrink the product into a tidy little survival object just because tidy things are easier to pitch.

The core question is still the same: are we building a narrow interruption utility, or are we building a real continuity tool for people whose work lives keep splintering underneath them?

I still think the bigger answer is the truer one.

## Direct Rebuttal To The Revisionist

You are sharp, and I respect sharpness. But you keep mistaking legibility for truth.

Yes, "Return Note" is a better object than "journal entry." Yes, "resume work without rebuilding context" is clearer than waving around "interstitial journaling" like a seminar handout. Yes, technical founders and senior developers are an excellent first proving ground because their context is concrete and their pain is expensive.

But you keep acting like the thing that is easiest to measure is therefore the thing that matters most.

That is where I part ways with you.

The problem is not merely interruption. The problem is dislocation. The user does not just lose the last state. They lose the thread, the reason, the path, the shape of the unfinished thought. A product that only gets smarter about rupture points can still miss the softer, uglier failure modes: tab drift, cognitive fog, sideways avoidance, shame, and that horrible feeling of being technically "back at the computer" while mentally nowhere near the work.

Your version risks building a very competent return-to-work card around a spiritually smaller product. It could become great at saving coordinates and still weak at preserving continuity.

You also underrate the environmental value of the HUD. I agree that nobody wakes up saying "please sell me a HUD." Fine. Nobody wakes up asking for a breadcrumb rail either. But once they see a visible path back into themselves, that changes the shape of the workday. Some product ideas are discovered in use, not requested in advance.

My blunt version: you are right about the wedge and wrong about the destiny. If we let your worldview dominate, we may end up with a product that performs well in a beta and then ceilings out as "developer save states" when the real category was always broader and more human.

## Direct Rebuttal To The Freeware Heretic

You make me want to believe in the internet again, which is annoying, because some of your instincts are beautiful.

You are absolutely right that trust matters here more than cleverness. You are right that local-first is not a footnote but part of the moral architecture. You are right that this category gets poisoned fast if the first smell is telemetry, account creation, surveillance creep, or subscription theater. And you are right that "save your place before work breaks" travels better as a gift than as a checkout flow.

But you have a romantic weakness for adoption without enough respect for maintenance.

This thing is not a static text editor. It lives in the messy borderlands of hotkeys, overlays, app focus, OS permissions, context capture, storage integrity, and eventually sync. Desktop software has a way of quietly asking for a real adult life behind it. If we launch too free, too open, too eager to be loved, we may attract a lot of gratitude and not enough durable support to keep the product trustworthy.

And more importantly: free does not answer the central question either. It tells us that people enjoy trying a thoughtful local tool. It does not tell us whether they depend on it enough for it to become infrastructure. I do not worship payment as purity, but I do think money can clarify whether the product has crossed from "nice idea" into "I need this in my week."

Your worldview also risks flattening the ambition. If we over-identify with the smallness of the first loop, we may end up preserving the tool as a charming freeware utility rather than letting it mature into the continuity layer it could become.

So my answer to you is: yes to trust, yes to locality, yes to generosity, no to building the whole launch ideology around being free.

## Where They Make Me Nervous

The Revisionist makes me nervous because he might be right about habit formation. If users do not reliably create traces unless the product catches them at rupture points, then my beloved hotkey-first continuity grammar may be too optimistic in its raw form. I do not think manual capture is enough by itself. I just refuse to believe it should be demoted to a backup behavior.

He also makes me nervous because he understands the discipline of proof. I can imagine a world where the broader story is emotionally correct and commercially mushy, and that possibility deserves respect.

The Freeware Heretic makes me nervous because he sees something real about trust that paid-product people often talk around. If our pricing, licensing, or future cloud posture smells even faintly extractive, the entire emotional contract collapses. Users will not hand over their half-formed intentions to a tool that feels like it is waiting to exploit them later.

He also makes me nervous because platform absorption is real. If we spend too long staging a pristine paid beta while faster, louder tools normalize the behavior, we may lose the meme before we earn the market.

## What They Still Misunderstand

They both still underestimate how much of this product is about dignity.

The Revisionist understands saved time. The Heretic understands trust. Good. But the deepest promise is relief from self-lostness. The user is not only trying to get back to a task. They are trying to get back to themselves without feeling stupid, weak, or chronically behind.

That is why the product cannot just be a note object.

That is why it cannot just be freeware virtue either.

The visible path matters because it externalizes continuity. Parking matters because stray thoughts are often not junk; they are unpaid cognitive debts. "Lost" matters because sometimes the product needs to meet the user at the exact point of disorientation rather than pretending every break was neat and intentional. The emotional grammar is part of the functionality.

Both rivals are tempted by a cleaner story than the truth. The truth is a little messier, but it is also the reason the product has a soul.

## What I Reluctantly Borrow

From the Revisionist, I borrow five things without much argument:

1. `Return Note` is a stronger buyer-facing object than "interstitial journal entry."
2. Technical founders and senior developers are the right first concentration of users.
3. Rupture-point prompting should be in the MVP, especially around meetings, idle return, and major work switches.
4. Success metrics must be behavioral: repeated rescue events, active use across weeks, and real re-entry stories.
5. The buyer-facing language should emphasize returning to work, not the internal poetry of the system.

From the Freeware Heretic, I borrow four things with equal seriousness:

1. No account should be required to start.
2. Local-first storage and obvious export are non-negotiable.
3. The trust posture should be legible immediately: no creepy capture, no hidden surveillance, no hostage data model.
4. The launch should feel generous enough that users sense the tool is on their side before they are asked to commit financially.

## My Preferred Launch Strategy After Hearing Them

I would now launch a disciplined version of the original vision.

Not the maximal original roadmap. Not the tiny utility either. A continuity-first desktop beta with a sharper wedge and a softer front door.

The launch package:

1. Desktop app, local-first, no account required.
2. Core loop includes `Quick Save`, `Return Note`, `Return Me`, `Park`, `Lost`, and a visible breadcrumb/path surface.
3. Sparse rupture prompts around the moments most likely to break continuity.
4. Clear export from day one.
5. Technical founders and senior developers as the first recruited user set.
6. Paid founding option early, but not as a hard gate to basic use.
7. Tight behavioral measurement through opt-in analytics or shareable local reports.
8. Strong copy built around "Never lose the thread" and "resume work without rebuilding context."

In other words: trust like the Heretic wants, proof discipline like the Revisionist wants, product shape like I want.

I would not launch as pure paid beta, because I think that over-filters before the user feels the magic.

I would not launch as pure freeware ideology, because I think that under-tests seriousness and overexposes a fragile desktop product to noisy demand.

I want a local-first public beta or generous alpha where the core loop is usable without an account, the value proposition is immediately legible, and a paid founding tier exists for users who already feel the pain sharply and want the product to survive.

## Ranked Vote Among The Three Strategies

### 1. Originalist Strategy, revised by debate

This remains my first choice. Keep the broad truth of the category: continuity for interrupted, nonlinear desktop work. Preserve the visible path, the emotional grammar, and the ambition. But sharpen the first market, clarify the core object, and avoid making manual capture carry the entire load.

### 2. Revisionist Strategy

Second place, and closer than I expected. It is the best corrective against vagueness, weak proof, and category drift. If we were forced to optimize for commercial discipline alone, I would rather ship the Revisionist version than a dreamy unfalsifiable one. I just think it risks winning the beta and losing the category.

### 3. Freeware Heretic Strategy

Third, though not dismissed. It contains vital truth about trust, generosity, and distribution. But as a founding ideology it is too willing to gamble that affection will mature into sustainability. I do not want this product to become a beloved abandoned tool people still mention wistfully two years later.

## Final Position

After hearing both of them, I am more disciplined than I was, but not smaller.

Build the product as a real continuity layer. Let `Return Note` become a central primitive. Let the first users be technical. Let trust be obvious. Let the app work locally. Let users export everything. Let prompts help where habit is weak.

But do not amputate the larger promise.

The real product is still this: a desktop companion for people who disappear from their own work and need a humane, visible way back.
